Main Idea

Older students with reading disabilities need more than standard instruction. This paper examines how increasing the intensity of reading interventions affects learning outcomes in upper elementary students.

Why this study matters

By upper elementary school, struggling readers are expected to learn from text, not just learn how to read. However, many students still have gaps in foundational skills such as decoding, fluency, and comprehension strategies. Traditional classroom instruction often does not address these gaps, making targeted and intensive intervention critical for helping these students catch up.

What does “intensity” mean?

Intervention intensity is not just about doing more of the same. It includes increasing instructional time, reducing group size, adding components, and delivering more explicit and targeted instruction.

However, research shows that simply increasing time or group size does not always lead to stronger outcomes for older students, highlighting the need to rethink how interventions are intensified. Key findings across three studies

Study 1

Less intensive intervention

Moderate gains in reading comprehension, especially for students with stronger baseline skills.

Study 2

More intensive intervention

Improved word reading accuracy and fluency, but limited effects on comprehension.

Study 3

Reading + mindset intervention

Small gains in decoding skills, but no added benefit from mindset training.

Overall

Intensity matters

Students with more severe difficulties require more intensive intervention to make progress.

What did we learn?

Increasing intensity helped students improve foundational skills such as word reading, but did not immediately translate into gains in reading comprehension.

This suggests that comprehension growth may require longer intervention duration or reaching a certain threshold of decoding ability before improvement occurs.

What about mindset?

Adding a growth mindset intervention did not lead to additional academic gains. The reading intervention itself was the primary driver of improvement.

This indicates that while motivation and beliefs may matter, they are not sufficient on their own without strong, explicit academic instruction.

Implications for practice

Effective intervention for older struggling readers requires more than increasing time. It requires careful design, explicit instruction, and alignment with students’ specific needs.

Even with intensive intervention, students often do not reach grade-level performance within one year, highlighting the need for sustained and possibly multi-year support.

Takeaway

Intensity is necessary but not sufficient. To truly accelerate reading outcomes for older struggling readers, interventions must be both intensive and strategically designed.
